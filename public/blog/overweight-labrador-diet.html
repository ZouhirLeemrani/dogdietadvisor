What to Feed an Overweight Labrador (Honest Guide)

Labs aren't overweight because their owners are careless. They're overweight because of genetics, household habits, and foods marketed as "healthy" that aren't. Here's what actually works.

Your Labrador is overweight. You've probably already noticed — a little rounder than they used to be, slightly less enthusiasm for stairs, maybe the vet mentioned it at the last checkup. You're not alone. Studies estimate that 59% of Labradors are overweight or obese. That's not a coincidence. There's a genuine biological reason Labs struggle with weight — and once you understand it, the solution becomes much clearer.

Why Labs Gain Weight So Easily (It's Not Your Fault)

In 2016, researchers at Cambridge University discovered that a significant percentage of Labrador Retrievers carry a genetic mutation in a gene called POMC — the pro-opiomelanocortin gene. This mutation impairs their brain's ability to register fullness after eating. In plain English: many Labs are genetically wired to always feel hungry, regardless of how much they've eaten.

This isn't a character flaw. Your Lab isn't being greedy. Their brain literally doesn't get the "I'm full" signal the way other dogs do. Which means standard feeding guidelines written for average dogs often result in overfeeding for Labs — and overfeeding in a breed already prone to weight gain leads exactly where you'd expect.

⚠️ Why this matters beyond appearanceEvery extra kilogram your Lab carries adds roughly 4x that weight in force on their joints with every step. A 5kg overweight Lab is putting 20kg of extra load on hips and knees that are already genetically predisposed to dysplasia. Weight loss isn't cosmetic for this breed — it's medical.

First: Is Your Lab Actually Overweight?

The scale isn't the best measure — body condition is. Use this simple physical check:

The 3-Point Body Condition Check

✓
Ribs: You should be able to feel each rib without pressing hard — like feeling the back of your hand. If you have to press firmly to find them, your dog is overweight.
✓
Waist: Looking down from above, you should see a visible narrowing behind the ribs. A straight or barrel shape means excess weight.
✓
Tuck: Looking from the side, the abdomen should tuck up slightly behind the ribcage. A flat or rounded underline is a sign of extra weight.
✗
If ribs are invisible and there's no waist: Your dog is likely overweight and a vet consultation is worthwhile to rule out thyroid issues before starting a diet.

The One Thing Most Owners Get Wrong

They switch to a "light" or "weight management" food — and then continue feeding the same volume they were feeding before.

Weight management formulas have fewer calories per cup. But if you pour the same sized bowl you always have, your dog might actually end up eating more total calories than before, because the reduced-calorie food requires a larger serving to meet daily nutrient needs. The label says so — the "recommended serving" for a lighter formula is often larger than for a standard formula.

The fix: weigh your dog's food by grams, not by cup volume. Cups are wildly inconsistent — a "cup" of kibble can vary by 20% depending on how you scoop it. A kitchen scale costs less than a bag of food and is the single most effective weight management tool you can buy.

📋 The actual calorie targets for a weight-loss LabA 35kg Lab at ideal weight needs roughly 1,400–1,600 kcal/day for maintenance. For active weight loss, reduce to 70–80% of that: 980–1,280 kcal/day. Check the kcal/kg value on your food bag and calculate from there. Aim for 1–2% body weight loss per week maximum.

The 8-Week Lab Weight Loss Plan

Switching food alone rarely produces results without a structured approach. Here's a realistic week-by-week plan:

📋 8-Week Labrador Weight Loss Plan

Week 1–2 Transition to new food gradually (25% new, 75% old → 50/50 → 75/25 → 100% new). Weigh your dog at the start. Buy a kitchen scale for food portions. Calculate daily calorie target at 75% of maintenance needs.
Week 3–4 Weigh your dog again. Expect 0.3–0.6kg loss for a 35kg Lab. If no change, reduce daily portions by 5–10%. Add 10 minutes to daily walks. Replace all treat calories with low-calorie alternatives (cucumber, carrot, apple slices).
Week 5–6 Check body condition using the rib test. Ribs becoming more palpable = you're on track. Increase exercise gradually — aim for 45–60 minutes total activity per day. Consider splitting meals into 3 smaller portions to reduce hunger between feeds.
Week 7–8 Weigh again and reassess. Total loss of 1.5–2.5kg for a 35kg overweight Lab is a healthy result at this stage. If you've hit this, maintain current portions. If still stagnant, consult your vet to rule out hypothyroidism.

Low-Calorie Treats That Won't Undo Your Work

Treats are where most Lab weight loss plans collapse. A Lab will work hard for any food reward — which is great for training and terrible for calorie counting. The solution isn't eliminating treats. It's replacing high-calorie treats with low-calorie ones:

  • Baby carrots — about 4 calories each, most Labs love them
  • Cucumber slices — virtually zero calories, good for hydration
  • Apple pieces (no seeds) — sweet enough to feel like a reward
  • Green beans (plain, no salt) — can also be used as a food volume extender in the bowl
  • Kibble from their daily portion — use 10% of their daily food as training treats, deducted from their meals

🚫 Treats that are silently sabotaging your Lab's dietCommercial dental chews (50–100 calories each), peanut butter in excess, cheese, bread, "lite" dog biscuits that still contain significant calories. The word "lite" on a treat means almost nothing — always check the calorie count per piece.

Exercise: What Actually Works for Overweight Labs

Exercise alone won't shift a Lab's weight — diet accounts for roughly 80% of weight loss. But the right exercise accelerates the process and prevents the muscle loss that comes with calorie restriction.

For an overweight Lab, avoid high-impact activities like repetitive ball chasing on hard ground — this puts massive stress on already-strained joints. Instead:

  • Swimming — zero impact, Labs love water, burns significant calories
  • Leash walking on grass or soft ground — consistent, manageable, joint-friendly
  • Sniff walks — slower pace but mentally stimulating, surprisingly tiring for dogs
  • Gentle fetch on grass — short sessions, soft surfaces only

Start at your dog's current fitness level and increase duration by 10% per week. An overweight Lab that's been sedentary shouldn't be suddenly running 5km — build up gradually to protect joints.

When to Talk to Your Vet

Most Lab weight gain is straightforward — too many calories, not enough movement. But sometimes something else is going on. Get a vet check if:

  • Your Lab gained weight rapidly without an obvious dietary change
  • They're lethargic beyond what you'd expect from being overweight
  • Their coat has become dull or they're shedding excessively
  • Weight loss isn't happening despite a genuine calorie reduction over 4+ weeks

These can be signs of hypothyroidism — a common condition in Labs that causes weight gain and slowed metabolism regardless of diet. It's easily diagnosed with a blood test and managed with daily medication.

The Bottom Line

An overweight Labrador isn't a hopeless case — it's a manageable one. The key variables are simpler than most articles make them sound:

  1. Switch to a weight management formula with controlled calories and high protein
  2. Weigh food by grams, not by cup
  3. Reduce treats to under 10% of daily calories, using low-calorie alternatives
  4. Add consistent low-impact exercise — swimming if possible
  5. Track weight weekly and adjust portions if progress stalls

Labs are motivated, resilient, and adaptable. The same food drive that caused the problem can be channeled into the solution — measured meals, vegetable treats as rewards, and an owner who understands that a slightly hungry Lab is a healthier Lab. You've got this.
